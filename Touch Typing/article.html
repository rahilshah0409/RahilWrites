Touch typing and its benefits that you never knew about

Having a desktop computer or a laptop is essential for the day to day lives of a lot of people. We may be forcing ourselves out of bed to watch that university lecture during the day or scrolling on YouTube for hours on end during the night. Either way, it is fair to say that life without that computer would be very different. How do we interact with this incredible device? Mostly by the keyboard and through the act of typing. So if we type so much, shouldn’t we at least be trying to type “better”? This was the question that came across my mind when I started to look into touch typing, and decided to dedicate time to learn and practise it last month.

What exactly is touch typing?

A good place to start this article is to get rid of some confusion some of you may be having as you read this. What is touch typing? Simply put, even though it may be hard to believe, there is a right and wrong way to type on a standard keyboard (also known as the QWERTY keyboard, which most of us are familiar with). Touch typing is the “right” style of typing. It is based on the principle that certain fingers should only press a small number of keys, putting an emphasis on all fingers being used to type English prose. Instead of searching for keys and only using your two index fingers to type (also known as the “hunt and peck” typing style), touch typing relies on using muscle memory and your knowledge of where each finger should be placed, removing the need to look at the keyboard while you type.

OK, but why did you get interested in this?

Now that you know what I decided to learn more about, you may be wondering why I even bothered practising in the first place. After all, it seems like quite a random thing to get involved in, so why bother? As you know, I study Computer Science, and as much as I hate to admit it, I spend A LOT of time typing. Whether it is programming or searching Google for something I don’t understand in my course (trust me- it happens a lot), the fact remains- I use my laptop for the majority of my day. Realising this simple fact started to spur an interest in using my laptop more efficiently. If I could find out a way to interact with my laptop more proficiently, not only would it feel great personally but I would also be able to reap the benefits of simply doing more in a certain period of time. With that, touch typing seemed to be a useful thing to start practising.

How exactly will touch typing benefit ME?

Some of you may go away from this article and forget all about touch typing within half an hour, and that’s fine. Nonetheless, some of you may want to see more. Doing more in less time is part of the story, but I quickly realised that there are more benefits that may not be obvious at first glance. I separate the advantages of touch typing in three main categories:

Time

Here is my version of a quick touch typing lesson. Through particular hand placement, you will start to adapt to the idea that different fingers are “responsible” for pressing a small selection of keys on the keyboard. To give a small example, your left pinky finger is responsible for pressing ‘1’, ‘q’, ‘a’, ‘z’, as well as the ‘Tab’, ‘Caps Lock’ and ‘Shift’ keys (some of you may be confused, realising that you never use your pinky finger to type at all. Don’t worry- neither did I until a few weeks ago). With this technique, each finger does less work and moves around the keyboard less, and this saves time. Time that accumulates overtime. It does this in two main ways. First, with enough practice, you will be able to simply type more words per minute. Second, you will become more accurate with your typing, so no more of slamming the backspace in anger after typing the same word incorrectly five times. Dread that essay you need to write or that email you just want to get out of the way? You could literally get it over with in less time.

Increased focus

Practising this style of typing is a productive thing to do for more reasons than simply saving time. An important part of touch typing, and something that was personally hard to grasp at first, is to beat the tendency to look at the keyboard and instead focus on the screen. The act of practising touch typing is all about building muscle memory so that your fingers are in the right place at the right time. Hence, typing becomes something that requires less brain power than it would have done when you spent time searching for keys to press. Looking at the keyboard and focusing on what you are pressing takes up more of your attention than you may think. The simple act of typing requires interaction from different parts of your brain, including those involved in strong motor skills and depicting language. With that said, by making the act of typing a more natural process that requires less thought, you free up mental capacity in your brain for other things, like focusing on the task at hand or coming up with more creative ideas.

Physical benefits

Aside from the productivity angle, touch typing brought benefits that I never really considered. The main idea I ignored is the improvement of your posture as you type. The hand placement that is encouraged to make touch typing an easier task requires you to sit with your back straight, shoulders back and neck naturally aligned. I am aware that the majority of people reading this don’t experience chronic back pain and hence think that this point is not really applicable to them. I thought the same. Having said that, being forced to sit in a particular way opened my eyes to how far forward my neck would get and how hunched over I was when sitting. Simply put, my posture was shockingly bad. However, being mindful of where I place my fingers and how I sit has made for a more comfortable experience overall.

I’ll try it out- but where do I start?

There may be a few of you out there that may decide to give this touch typing thing a go. If that is you, I would like to give a few tips that I have found helpful when it has come to learning and practising touch typing:

  • Accuracy before speed- you may feel the tendency to type as fast as you can. Despite this urge (which I still feel on a daily basis), try to focus on accuracy of typing more than the speed, even if that means typing slower than what you would like. For a couple of weeks not too long ago, I dropped down to typing 30 words per minute (which is not fast at all) to focus on accuracy. Whilst annoying at first, you will reap the benefits gradually overtime.
  • Daily practise- at least when you begin to learn touch typing, I would recommend regular practice for a shorter period of time. For me, building the consistency has helped to develop the muscle memory that is required for certain hand placements to feel natural.
  • Take it as far as you want- just because you may want to start learning does not mean that you should feel pressured to aim for absolute perfection. For example, I still make mistakes with which finger I use for certain keys, but I am happy with the changes that I have made with my typing habits as of now.

It is worth putting out this disclaimer- I’m not an expert who types super fast or super accurately, and I don’t want to teach you nor persuade you. Instead, if there is one thing that I want to get out of this article, it is to bring across the point that there are many ways one could try to make more use of the time that they have, even if it is as simple as learning how to type faster.

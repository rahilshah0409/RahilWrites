Is it better to be a generalist or specialist?

You have found yourself at the start of this article, and some of you may already be a bit confused. Generalist? Specialist? What do these terms actually mean, especially in the context of the topic matter of this article? A generalist is formally defined as:

“a person whose knowledge, aptitudes, and skills are applied to a field as a whole or to a variety of different fields”.

If there is someone in your life that you think does a bit of everything, then they are what you would call a generalist. They may be academically smart, but also play the piano well, but also are the best footballer you know.

A specialist, on the other hand, is formally defined as:

“a person who devotes himself or herself to one subject or to one particular branch of a subject or pursuit”.

This refers to someone that is exceptional at one thing because of spending a lot of their time on it. We may know that one person who spends all their free time in the gym and is really physically fit, or someone who only seems to do maths problems in their spare time and seems mathematically gifted. They are specialists.

The question of whether you should be a generalist or specialist may hold personal meaning depending on who you are. I believe that this fundamental question spills and feeds into a lot of different aspects of life, from the mindset that drives you daily to the values and morals you have as a human being. It is one that has been on my mind a lot over the last couple of months. I would like to get involved in as much as I could in secondary school, whether it be mathematics or sport or art or drama. However, coming to university, I gradually came up to a fork in the road, constantly debating on whether I should continue this generalist mentality or focus more on my course and career. It was a debate that drove me insane at times and may be one you are thinking about too.

Another name for a generalist is a “Renaissance Man”, a name that comes from the fact that there were many men and women of this nature in the Renaissance period. It was, in fact, the generalists that were respected and held in high regard in societies as early as the Romans. An example of such a person is Leonardo Di Vinci. Widely recognised for being the famous artist responsible for the Mona Lisa, Di Vinci also made great contributions to the world of mathematics and astronomy. An engineer who also had great interests in literature and botany. This is just the start of the long list of interests he had and fields he was involved in. Another example is Nikola Tesla- a humanitarian, inventor, mathematician and electrical engineer. These people, at the time, were highly respected in almost all the fields they took interest in and it is fair to say that they weren’t specialists.

Do you know anyone with the same kind of life as Di Vinci or Tesla? My guess: maybe not. But why is this? As discussed in Zach Jones’ Ted talk, modern society has developed into one where specialisation is promoted, and generalisation is seen more as the exception rather than the rule. Take the education system we find ourselves in. We go from nursery to primary school, and then to secondary school to take our GCSEs. We are then told to pick three or four subjects to pursue further, before typically being told to choose one course to study at university two years later (I am aware that not all young people take this path, but this is a path commonly taken). We go through university and develop skills that lead us into a particular role in a particular industry.

This path of life can be considered by some as unidirectional and specialist, but there is good reason for this structure. The problems that we face as a society and the progress that we have made (scientific, industrial or otherwise) requires detailed knowledge in certain areas. This is where specialists thrive. Going into depth in one or a selected few things allows them to come incredibly good or knowledgeable at it. This can bring a lot of personal and societal benefits, from breakthroughs in fields that improve our understanding of the world to becoming a famous musician. Take Michael Jordan for example. In the Netflix documentary “The Last Dance”, viewers get an insight into Jordan’s winning mentality and his amazing work ethic that was fuelled by his sheer and undisturbed love for the game of basketball, resulting in him becoming one of the best players of all time.

Nonetheless, living life as a specialist does come with its drawbacks, and society being centred around specialisation does not mean that generalisation has no place. While focusing on depth in which one delves into their craft, a specialist sacrifices the breadth that come with exploring several interests. In the context of a career, doors are open for a generalist in comparison to a specialist- credit to the development of adaptability to differing scenarios. Furthermore, the skills and abilities of a generalist may prove to be necessary soon. Speaking on a larger scale, the knowledge and experience that one gains from exploring different areas allows the bridging of seemingly unrelated ideas; crucial in making progress to solve the world’s most pressing future problems that involve existential risk (risk involving the existence of humanity) like AI safety and nuclear warfare.

Considering whether you should strive to be a generalist or a specialist is a question of depth VS breadth, and how many differing hats you are willing to wear and add to your identity. It is important to realise that in today’s constantly evolving society, great gain usually comes when both kinds of people collaborate with one another. Nonetheless, the question still remains.

Personally, I have been influenced by many views on the topic and have boiled the gathered insight down into a view which I urge you to consider. This is a concept introduced to me by Tim Ferris, who was asked to answer the same question. Through the course of this article, I have expressed the nature of being specialist and being generalist as though they are two completely opposing ways to live your life. However, there does exist a middle ground where both concepts coexist. This is explained by Ferris as being a “specialised generalist” or the “layering of skills” as the author and entrepreneur James Clear puts it. This entails realising unrelated interests and finding a way to combine these distinct ideas to create a (potentially unique) niche that you can pursue in depth. This is applicable in the context of a career or someone simply pursuing a hobby/interest, and I found that it was an interesting perspective to take. An example of a “specialised generalist” is Scott Adams, someone who’s interest in drawing and experience in business created the Dilbert comic strip; a comic known for its sarcastic office humour.

While this article provides one option to ponder upon, it is important to mention that there is no answer that suits everyone. Some may argue that there is no concrete answer at all- there should be no preference over the two. Others may bring up the compelling argument that different times in your life may call for different approaches, and there is not one answer that can serve you for the rest of your life. This is a view that I agree with to some extent. Nonetheless, I’d love to know what you all think.
